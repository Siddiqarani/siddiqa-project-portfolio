# Next Generation Sequencing (NGS) Workflow

## Overview
Next Generation Sequencing (NGS) is a high-throughput sequencing technology that allows millions to billions of DNA fragments to be sequenced simultaneously. Compared to traditional Sanger sequencing, NGS significantly reduces sequencing time and cost while increasing throughput.

This project explains the **complete NGS workflow**, from sample preparation to computational analysis.

## Objectives
- Understand the sequencing process used in NGS
- Study the computational analysis pipeline
- Explore major applications of high-throughput sequencing

## NGS Workflow

### 1. Sample Collection and Purification
DNA or RNA samples are collected and purified to remove contaminants.

### 2. Library Preparation
DNA fragments are generated and sequencing adapters are attached.

### 3. Clonal Amplification
DNA fragments are amplified to form clusters on the sequencing flow cell.

### 4. Sequencing by Synthesis
Fluorescent nucleotides are incorporated during sequencing and detected by imaging systems.

### 5. Data Processing
Raw sequencing reads are processed through computational pipelines.

## Bioinformatics Analysis Pipeline

Typical computational steps include:

1. Quality control
2. Demultiplexing
3. Read alignment
4. Variant calling
5. Post-processing
6. Variant annotation
7. Data visualization

These steps transform raw sequencing data into biologically meaningful information such as genetic variants and mutations. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

## Applications
NGS is widely used in:

- Cancer genomics
- Rare disease diagnosis
- Population genetics
- Microbial genomics
- Drug discovery

## Advantages
- High throughput
- Faster sequencing
- Lower cost per base
- High sensitivity for detecting rare variants

## Conclusion
NGS technologies have revolutionized genomics by enabling rapid, large-scale sequencing and analysis of DNA and RNA.
